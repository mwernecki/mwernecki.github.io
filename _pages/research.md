# PhD thesis (in preparation)

My doctoral research, conducted at the Department of Microbiology at the University of Wrocław, explores how bacteria adapt when the stressor is not strong enough to halt growth but still sufficient to induce stress. In this case, the stressor is sub-inhibitory concentrations of silver nanoparticles.

To examine this, we repeatedly transfer *E. coli* cultures exposed to low levels of silver nanoparticles and allow them to grow for hundreds of generations. This is a simplified model of what may occur in a heavily polluted environment or in an infected wound treated with a silver nanoformulation.

I then observe how these populations change in terms of both phenotype and genotype. To analyse phenotypes, we use time-lapse imaging of colonies on plates to extract growth parameters at the single-colony level[^1]. As doing this manually would be impractical, we use a machine learning model to annotate individual colonies. For genomes, we look at SNPs, structural variants, and potential changes in methylation patterns using nanopore sequencing.

Our broad goal is to connect growth parameters with genomic changes and to understand how bacterial variability develops under long-term nanoparticle stress.

[^1]: The concept comes from prior works: *Bär, J. et al. Efficient microbial colony growth dynamics quantification with ColTapp, an automated image analysis application. [Sci Rep 10, 16084 (2020).](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-020-72979-4)* and *Levin-Reisman, I. et al. Automated imaging with ScanLag reveals previously undetectable bacterial growth phenotypes. [Nat Methods 7, 737–739 (2010).](https://doi.org/10.1038/nmeth.1485)*
